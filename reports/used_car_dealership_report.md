# Used Car Market Analysis
### What Buyers Value — and What It Means for Your Inventory

**Prepared for:** Used Car Dealership
**Date:** February 2026
**Analysis based on:** 384,590 used car listings across the United States

---

## Executive Summary

A data analysis of nearly 400,000 used car listings was conducted to identify which vehicle attributes most strongly influence the price buyers are willing to pay. Six factors account for the majority of price variation in the market: vehicle age, mileage, title status, engine size, drive type, and condition. A predictive pricing model was built from this data that explains approximately 60% of why one vehicle is priced differently from another. The findings below translate directly into acquisition and pricing decisions that can improve margin and reduce time on the lot.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Vehicle Age Is the Biggest Price Driver
Newer vehicles command significantly higher prices. Each additional year of age reduces a vehicle's expected market value in a measurable and consistent way. Buyers are acutely sensitive to model year — even a one or two year difference at the newer end of the market has a noticeable impact on what they will pay.

**Recommendation:** Prioritise acquiring recent model-year inventory. The return on newer vehicles is stronger and more predictable.

---

### 2. Mileage Matters — Especially at the Lower End
Low mileage vehicles carry a clear price premium. The relationship is non-linear: the difference between 10,000 and 40,000 miles has a greater impact on price than the difference between 80,000 and 110,000 miles. Buyers respond most strongly to vehicles that have been lightly used.

**Recommendation:** Where possible, seek listings under 50,000 miles. These vehicles price higher and sell faster.

---

### 3. Title Status Has an Outsized Impact
Of all the attributes analysed, parts-only and salvage titles are the most damaging to price — more so than poor condition or high mileage. Buyers apply a steep discount to any vehicle with a compromised title, regardless of its actual mechanical state.

**Recommendation:** Only acquire clean-title vehicles. The acquisition savings on a salvage or rebuilt title do not compensate for the price penalty buyers impose at resale.

---

### 4. Engine Size Influences Price
Vehicles with larger engines — V6 and V8 — consistently sell for more than equivalent four-cylinder vehicles. This reflects strong buyer demand for trucks, SUVs, and performance-oriented vehicles in the upper price tiers.

**Recommendation:** When acquiring trucks and SUVs, favour V6 and V8 configurations. Four-cylinder options in these segments are harder to price competitively.

---

### 5. Drive Type Adds Value
Four-wheel drive (4WD) and all-wheel drive (AWD) vehicles are priced above front-wheel drive equivalents across all vehicle types. Buyers associate 4WD and AWD with versatility and safety, and are willing to pay for it.

**Recommendation:** Stock a higher proportion of AWD and 4WD models. These move at a premium and are in consistent demand.

---

### 6. Condition Affects the Bottom Line
Vehicles in good or like-new condition command a clear price premium over those listed in fair condition. Salvage condition vehicles are priced at the bottom of the market — the discount buyers expect is substantial.

**Recommendation:** Invest in reconditioning vehicles to at least a "good" condition standard before listing. The uplift in expected sale price typically justifies the cost.

---

## Inventory Recommendations at a Glance

| Decision | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **What to prioritise** | Newer model years, low mileage, clean title, good or like-new condition |
| **What to avoid** | Salvage, rebuilt, or parts-only titles — the price penalty exceeds acquisition savings |
| **Engine preference** | V6 and V8 for trucks and SUVs; four-cylinders are harder to move at margin |
| **Drive type** | AWD and 4WD over FWD where options are comparable |
| **What does not matter** | Paint colour and state of origin have negligible impact on price |
| **Pricing check** | Compare any listing against the model's prediction — if you are significantly above it for a given age, mileage, and title, expect longer days on lot |

---

## A Note on Model Confidence

The pricing model used in this analysis explains approximately **60% of why vehicle prices differ** from one listing to the next. This is a strong result for a market as varied as used cars. The remaining 40% reflects factors that are not captured in listing data — service history, interior condition, local demand, and seller motivation.

The model should be used as a **reference and cross-check**, not as a guarantee. It is most reliable for everyday vehicles (sedans, SUVs, trucks) in the common price range. For luxury, exotic, or highly specialised vehicles, apply your own market knowledge alongside the model output.

Market conditions shift over time. As fuel prices, interest rates, and consumer preferences change, so will the relative importance of these factors. This analysis reflects the current dataset and should be revisited as new data becomes available.

---

*Analysis conducted using a Ridge Regression model with polynomial features trained on 307,672 listings and validated on a held-out set of 76,918 listings.*
